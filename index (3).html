The Work

From architecture
to urban intelligence

I trained as an architect at BMS College of Engineering before going to the Bartlett School of Architecture at UCL, where my master's thesis — IsoChronic City — applied machine learning, space syntax, and generative design to rethink what the 15-minute neighbourhood could mean for a post-pandemic London.

It won the gold medal at WASA-WLS 2022, the MAB23 Student Award, and was featured in ArchDaily and ArchiDiaries. But more than the recognition, it convinced me that the most powerful design decisions are made at the data level — before a line is drawn.

Now

Same tools.
Different city.

The methodologies developed at UCL — spatial computation, machine learning, network analysis — were built on London. The next question is whether they hold anywhere else. Bangalore is the case study: a rapidly growing city with vast amounts of unread data and very little applied urban intelligence.

I'm building a real-time sensing instrument to collect ground-truth urban data, developing Python automation tools for AEC workflows, and writing a series of spatial analyses that apply the same rigour I used at the Bartlett to a city that hasn't been looked at this way.

Everything is work in progress. That's the point.
